# Case Study: The Mechanics of a Phishing Attack

## Definition and Scope
Phishing is a prevalent form of social engineering where attackers impersonate a legitimate institution to steal sensitive data. While it can take many forms, including SMS (smishing) or voice calls (vishing), email remains the primary medium for targeting university students. The goal is typically to harvest credentials or install malware by exploiting human psychology rather than technical vulnerabilities.

## Step-by-Step Execution
1. **The Hook:** An attacker sends a mass email appearing to be from a trusted source, such as the University Registrar. The subject line often creates a sense of urgency, like "Unpaid Tuition Fees – Immediate Action Required."
2. **The Deception:** The email contains a link to a fraudulent payment portal. This site is designed to look identical to the official university gateway, complete with correct logos, fonts, and "secure" padlocks to build false trust.
3. **The Harvest:** Once the student enters their login credentials or credit card information, the data is captured by the attacker’s backend script.
4. **The Exploitation:** The attacker uses this data to drain bank accounts or sell the student’s identity on the dark web. They may also use the compromised student account to launch internal attacks on the university network.

## Targets and Consequences
Students are primary targets due to their high digital activity and reliance on online portals for academic and financial management. The consequences are severe, ranging from immediate financial loss to long-term identity theft. Furthermore, a compromised account can lead to a student being locked out of essential academic resources, resulting in missed deadlines and significant stress.
